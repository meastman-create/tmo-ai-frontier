AEO measurement tooling matured materially this week. The question has shifted from "should we care about AI visibility" to "what's our score right now and what do we do about it." Two free tools now make that answer immediate.

active HubSpot AEO Sensor: Free AI Visibility Dashboard GA highest TMO relevance
HubSpot launched AEO Sensor on May 14, 2026 — a free, public dashboard with no login required. It tracks three data streams: daily answer engine volatility scores (based on mention rate, citation rate, and AI-referred traffic across ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity); weekly AI-referred traffic trend estimates; and weekly visibility benchmarks by industry. Notably, HubSpot's own network data showed ChatGPT generating its lowest business referral traffic in 12 months during April 2026 — not because AI search is losing reach, but because AI is answering more questions without generating a click. Paid HubSpot AEO ($50/mo standalone) gives brand-level tracking; AEO Sensor gives the industry-level context to know whether a score shift is market-wide or brand-specific.
→ TMO AEO Sensor is the industry-level read; paid HubSpot AEO is the TMO-specific score. Use Sensor immediately to benchmark the "financial software" and "lending software" category volatility — if AEO Sensor shows high volatility in the category, TMO is not the only brand losing ground. That context changes the urgency framing for the investment conversation.
watch HubSpot AEO Content Actions: June 2026 Ship
HubSpot announced that "Content Actions" — the ability to publish directly from the AEO interface to act on visibility recommendations — is shipping June 2026. Today, HubSpot AEO identifies visibility gaps and recommends content types, but execution requires switching out of AEO into content creation tools. The June ship closes the loop. For Marketing Hub Pro and Enterprise users, this makes HubSpot AEO a complete cycle: measure visibility → receive recommendation → publish content → re-measure. This is what separates HubSpot AEO from standalone tools like Profound.
→ TMO If TMO is on Marketing Hub Pro or Enterprise, Content Actions in June eliminates the argument for a multi-tool AEO stack. Decision point: commit to HubSpot AEO before June ship or continue evaluating alternatives (Profound, Microsoft Clarity Citations). The June ship makes the single-platform case substantially stronger.
active Microsoft Clarity Citations Dashboard: GA
Microsoft Clarity moved its Citations dashboard to general availability this week. The feature gives publishers visibility into how AI systems retrieve, evaluate, and cite their content before users visit the site — measuring page citations, share of authority, AI referral traffic, grounding queries, and cited pages inside AI-generated responses. This is a free alternative to HubSpot AEO Sensor for citation-level tracking, without the industry benchmark component. The broader signal: measurement tooling for AEO is now as mature as SEO analytics was in 2015. The brands that move fastest in the next 60 days are building lasting citation advantages.
watch ChatGPT Referral Traffic Decline: What It Actually Means
HubSpot's AEO Sensor launch data is anchored on a counterintuitive finding: ChatGPT sent its lowest business referral traffic in 12 months during April 2026. Simultaneously, AI-referred traffic converts at 4–23x the rate of organic search. The explanation is structural: AI search is delivering more complete answers, reducing the percentage of queries that generate a click — but the queries that do click through represent higher buyer intent. The implication for B2B software brands is that the right KPI is citation share and answer sentiment, not traffic volume from AI sources. Traditional web analytics dashboards are measuring the wrong thing.

Compliance & Regulatory AI
moat narrative
▼

Two major compliance signals converged this week: Colorado's AI Act was effectively neutralized while CFPB Reg B's July 21 effective date accelerates toward lenders. The federal-state tension is live — state AGs are coordinating under Chopra while federal enforcement is pulling back. The net effect is asymmetric risk: lenders who let fair lending governance lapse now face state AG exposure on a 5-year statute of limitations.

resolved Colorado AI Act (SB 24-205) Effectively Neutralized high TMO relevance
Colorado SB 26-189 — the replacement for SB 24-205 — passed the legislature 56–7 on May 9, 2026, and is awaiting Governor Polis's signature. The replacement narrows the original law from a comprehensive algorithmic discrimination framework to a narrow transparency and notice requirement, eliminating the annual review, impact assessment, and outcome-reporting requirements that had generated broad industry opposition. The April 27 enforcement stay (resulting from xAI's constitutional challenge and DOJ intervention) means SB 24-205 will not be enforced on its June 30 effective date regardless. The DOJ intervention marked the first time the federal government has sought to invalidate a state AI law — establishing a precedent for federal preemption that will shape similar laws in Illinois, Texas, and California.
→ TMO If Polis signs SB 26-189, the compliance documentation bar for algorithmic lending tools drops significantly from what SB 24-205 would have required. Update the compliance framing for ProPilot and Pulse: the immediate Colorado risk is resolved, but state AG enforcement under existing ECOA/FHA theories is unaffected and actually intensifying through Chopra's DAGA coordination. → see Granola / May 14 AI Council.
threat CFPB Reg B: July 21 Effective Date Approaching
The CFPB's April 22, 2026 final rule amending Regulation B takes effect July 21, 2026. Three key changes: (1) disparate impact analysis eliminated as grounds for CFPB enforcement actions; (2) fair lending protections extended to business borrowers, not just consumers; (3) special purpose credit programs (SPCPs) cannot target beneficiaries by race or ethnicity. The first change benefits lenders relying on AI underwriting models that produce statistical disparities without proven discriminatory intent. The second change is potentially meaningful for TMO's government agency and CDFI customers who also serve business borrowers. Critically: state attorneys general can still pursue disparate impact claims under the Fair Housing Act (2-year statute) and ECOA (5-year statute) — the federal rollback does not close state exposure.
→ TMO Brief leadership before July 21 on what changed and what didn't. The short version: CFPB enforcement posture is looser but state AG enforcement is coordinating under Chopra's playbook at DAGA. For TMO's regulated-buyer positioning (CDFIs, government agencies), the compliance story is now "we maintain governance standards that exceed the current federal floor" — a stronger moat narrative than baseline compliance.
threat State AG Coordination Under Chopra Intensifying
Former CFPB Director Rohit Chopra, now advising the Democratic Attorneys General Association's Consumer Protection & Affordability Working Group, has been building coordinated AI enforcement strategies across state AGs using shared data and legal theories. While federal enforcement of disparate impact is contracting, state-level coordination is expanding. The practical consequence: lenders who rely on federal regulatory rollback to reduce AI governance overhead are accumulating multi-year statute-of-limitations exposure that could be triggered by a future federal administration or private plaintiff action.
